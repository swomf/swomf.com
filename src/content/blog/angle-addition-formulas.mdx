To find out $sin(\alpha+\beta)$, let's build a diagram of
what we're actually, trigonometrically doing.

  <div>
    Let's make a triangle with angle $\beta$ on it.
    
    If its hypotenuse is some number $H$, then:  
    $opposite=H \bullet sin(\beta)$  
    $adjacent=H \bullet cos(\beta)$

    Keep this in mind for later.

  </div>
  <img src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-02.svg" alt="step 2" class="mx-auto"/>
  <div>
    On top of its hypotenuse, let's add a triangle with angle $\alpha$ on it.

    Using the same intuition from $\triangle\beta$,
    we could figure out its sides, given its hypotenuse...

  </div>
  <img src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-03.svg" alt="step 3" class="mx-auto"/>
  <div>
    What's this? Just by stacking triangles, we've made a
    third triangle out of $\alpha + \beta$! Let's just draw
    a dotted line so that we realize it's there.

    Remember the intuition we talked about, where all we
    need is the hypotenuse to find the sides?

  </div>
  <img src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-04.svg" alt="step 4" class="mx-auto"/>
  <div>
    Let's set the hypotenuse of $\triangle(\alpha+\beta)$
    to $1$. This way, if we find its sides, we'll have
    *both*:  
  
    $1 \bullet cos(\alpha+\beta)$  
    $1 \bullet sin(\alpha+\beta)$

    Immaculate.

  </div>
  <img src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-05.svg" alt="step 5" class="mx-auto"/>
  <div>
    However, when we extend the logic to $\triangle\beta$,
    we notice that our goal, the sides of
    $\triangle(\alpha+\beta)$, is still out of reach.

    Now, we have to get *crafty.* Let's get the *very top angle*.

  </div>
  <img src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-06.svg" alt="step 6" class="mx-auto"/>
  <div>
    If we think back to geometry, we can draw a line
    parallel to the bottom line to discover a secret 4th
    right triangle. Parallel lines crossed by a
    transversal!

    Now all we do is hop between $\beta$ and $90-\beta$
    to get to the top.

  </div>
  <img src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-07.svg" alt="step 7" class="mx-auto"/>
  <div>
    With $\angle\beta$ and the hypotenuse $sin(\alpha)$,
    we solve for the sides of our 4th triangle...  
    
    And just like that, we have the final things we
    need to solve for $\triangle(\alpha+\beta)$.
  </div>
  <img src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-08.svg" alt="step 8" class="mx-auto"/>
  <div>
    Let's do addition and subtraction!

    $cos(\alpha+\beta)=cos\alpha cos\beta - sin\alpha sin\beta$

    $sin(\alpha+\beta)=sin\alpha cos\beta + cos\alpha sin\beta$

    For the basic angle-addition formulas, $Q.E.D.$~

## Not tired yet? Let's use what we have to derive the others!

The other formulas simply build off of these two.
Just remember that sine waves are odd functions and
cosine waves are even functions.

<img
  src="/blog-pictures/angle-addition-formulas-09.svg"
  alt="sine and cosine evenness"
  class="mx-auto"
/>

$$
\begin{align}
sin(-\theta) &= -sin(\theta) \notag \\
cos(-\theta) &= cos(\theta) \notag
\end{align}
$$

With this, the angle-subtraction formulas come in easy.

$$
\begin{align}
cos(\alpha + -\beta) &= cos(\alpha) cos(\beta) - sin(\alpha) sin(-\beta) \notag \\
cos(\alpha - \beta)  &= cos\alpha cos\beta - sin\alpha (-sin\beta) \notag \\
cos(\alpha - \beta)  &= cos\alpha cos\beta + sin\alpha sin\beta \notag \\
\notag \\
sin(\alpha + -\beta) &= sin(\alpha) cos(-\beta) + cos(\alpha) sin(-\beta) \notag \\
sin(\alpha - \beta)  &= sin\alpha cos\beta - cos\alpha sin\beta \notag \\
\end{align}
$$

And since the tangent of an angle is just $sin\theta \div cos\theta$,
tangent comes in easy too.

$$
\begin{align}
tan(\alpha + \beta) &= \frac{
  sin(\alpha + \beta)
}{
  cos(\alpha + \beta)
} \notag \\
tan(\alpha + \beta) &= \frac{
  sin\alpha cos\beta + cos\alpha sin\beta
}{
  cos\alpha cos\beta - sin\alpha sin\beta
} \notag \\
tan(\alpha + \beta) &= \frac{
  (cos\alpha cos\beta)(
    \frac{sin\alpha cos\beta}{cos\alpha cos\beta} +
    \frac{cos\alpha sin\beta}{cos\alpha cos\beta})
}{
  (cos\alpha cos\beta)(
    \frac{cos\alpha cos\beta}{cos\alpha cos\beta} -
    \frac{sin\alpha sin\beta}{cos\alpha cos\beta}
  )
} \notag \\
tan(\alpha + \beta) &= \frac{
  tan\alpha - tan\beta
}{
  1 + tan\alpha tan\beta
} \notag
\end{align}
$$

Though we could do this same procedure with $sin(\alpha - \beta)$
and $cos(\alpha - \beta)$, it's easier to recognize that tangents are even functions.

$$
\begin{align}
tan(-\theta) &= -tan(\theta) \notag \\
tan(\alpha + -\beta) &= \frac{
  tan\alpha - tan(-\beta)
}{
  1 + tan\alpha tan(-\beta)
} \notag \\
tan(\alpha - \beta) &= \frac{
  tan\alpha + tan\beta
}{
  1 - tan\alpha tan\beta
} \notag
\end{align}
$$
